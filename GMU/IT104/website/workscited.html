Works Cited

Douglas, J. (2011). Wooden Reels and the Maintenance of Virtual Life: Gaming and the Death Drive in a Digital Age. English Studies in Canada, 37(1), 85-106. Retrieved from https://search.proquest.com/docview/1010274189?accountid=14541

This article discusses the philosophical meaning of video games and why people play them. It looks into the concept of death and how it relates to gaming. Refers to writings of philosophers and how they pertain to virtual gaming. This also discusses some social repercussions of video games, as well as explores some possible reasons as to why people play them.

Gray, K., & Huang, W. (2015). More than addiction: Examining the role of anonymity, endless narrative, and socialization in prolonged gaming and instant messaging practices. Journal of Comparative Research in Anthropology and Sociology, 6(1), 133-147. Retrieved from https://search.proquest.com/docview/1712852742?accountid=14541

This study explores a user's prolonged engagement within virtual gaming communities. Compares said engagement with addiction and provides an alternative perspective into virtual addiction focusing on related themes. Examines two different groups' experience with both console and PC based multiplayer environments and how the factors relate to the use within the communities.

Kocurek, C. (2015). Adapting Violence: Death Race and the History of Gaming Moral Panic. In Coin-Operated Americans: Rebooting Boyhood at the Video Game Arcade (pp. 67-90). University of Minnesota Press. Retrieved from http://www.jstor.org/stable/10.5749/j.ctt19704vf.7

This chapter discusses a court case regarding video games. Then it talks about violent movies and other media and how they relate to arcade games. It goes on to discuss ethics in video games and the public's view of morality because of them.

Kocurek, C. (2015). Anarchy in the Arcade: Regulating Coin-Op Video Games. In Coin-Operated Americans: Rebooting Boyhood at the Video Game Arcade (pp. 91-114). University of Minnesota Press. Retrieved from http://www.jstor.org/stable/10.5749/j.ctt19704vf.8

This chapter lists efforts throughout the 70s and 80s to regulate the spread of coin-operated video games and arcades. It also connects the efforts to existing regulatory regimens and argues that the emergent technology of video gaming and the games' appeal to youths revived efforts in a lot of communities. The chapter note how the ills ascribed to video game arcades reflect both older anxieties about delinquency in young people alongside newer anxieties about technological changes happening around the nation. Discusses whether or not the policing of video games arises from the debate about what activities should rightly be included as a part of childhood.

Walsh, M. (2010). Mortal Combat: Video game makers fight for their right to be as bad as they wanna be. ABA Journal, 96(11), 22-23. Retrieved from http://www.jstor.org/stable/20789781

This news article discusses a law regarding video games. It also talks about how throughout history, there has always been something that challenges the public's view on what is morally right or wrong, like how video games are at the time. It then briefly talks about what exactly a video game is considered to be. Next, it shows how the law was challenged. The article then ends with speculation on whether or not judges will play video games themselves so they could accurately judge them for the sake of the law.

Weger, U. W., Loughnan, S., Sharma, D., & Gonidis, L. (2015). Virtually compliant: Immersive video gaming increases conformity to false computer judgments. Psychonomic Bulletin & Review, 22(4), 1111-1116. Retrieved from https://search.proquest.com/docview/1700288061?accountid=14541

This article explores whether role-playing video gaming causes people to identify with computers enough to trust a computer's judgments more than their own. Implications for human-computer interactions and the understanding of the formation of identity and self-concept are also discussed.
